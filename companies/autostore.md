# AutoStore
**Vertical:** Warehouse / fulfillment automation | **URL:** https://www.autostoresystem.com | **HQ:** Nedre Vats, Norway | **Founded:** 1996 | **Stage/Funding:** Public — Oslo Stock Exchange (Euronext) IPO Oct 2021 at ~$12.4B valuation; market cap ~$3.8B as of Feb 2026

## Product
AutoStore sells a cube-storage automated storage and retrieval system (ASRS): an aluminum grid in which bins stack floor-to-ceiling and battery-powered robots traverse the top surface to retrieve or store bins on demand. The system eliminates warehouse aisles, claiming 4× the storage density of conventional shelving and 99.7% uptime. It is sold exclusively through certified integration partners, never direct. Core hardware is the Grid, R5/R5+ robots, and ports/workstations; software includes the Router WES, Natural Slotting AI, and a cloud management layer (ISO/IEC 27001). Recent additions include CarouselAI (AI-powered robotic picker), AutoCase (full-case movement), FlexBins (variable bin sizes), and a Frozen-Only Grid configuration.

## ICP
Large and mid-large enterprises in e-commerce, omnichannel retail, grocery, pharma, and 3PL. Geography is global (deployed in ~60 countries) with concentration in Europe and North America. Typical deal size is six figures and above in capex; smaller installs can reach low-to-mid six figures, but the system is capital-intensive. Buyers are heads of supply chain / operations at companies needing high-density storage in constrained footprints.

## Traction
~1,700 systems live across ~60 countries; ~80,000 robots deployed. Annual revenue: ~$539M trailing twelve months as of end-2025 (vs. ~$601M reported for 2024 — some source variance). Revenue grew from ~$182M (2020) to ~$300M (2021) to ~$500M+ (2022), then slowed. Named in TIME Best Inventions 2025. Sources: Robotics & Automation News (Dec 2025); techinvestments.io; IPO filings (2021).

## Tech moat
Grid geometry and robot-on-top-of-grid design are protected by a significant patent portfolio — AutoStore has litigated these patents aggressively (vs. Ocado, Attabotics). Manufacturing is outsourced but integration partners are certified, creating a controlled distribution moat. Network effects are weak; data moat is modest. Primary defensibility is the patent estate plus 25+ years of deployment data feeding slotting/routing algorithms.

## Distribution model
100% channel: certified system integrators (SIs) handle sales, installation, and ongoing support. AutoStore trains and certifies partners but does not sell direct. Partners include large logistics integrators globally. Motion is largely enterprise/direct-to-SI, with the SI owning the customer relationship. No RaaS or subscription model publicly offered at scale.

## Critical weakness
The system only handles bins up to 30 kg; it cannot store oversized, irregularly shaped, or full-pallet goods, limiting addressable SKU range. Deep-stack retrieval latency grows as bin depth increases. The channel-only model means AutoStore has no direct customer relationship and limited visibility into real utilisation data. Revenue growth has decelerated sharply since 2022, suggesting saturation among large-enterprise early adopters and high price sensitivity preventing penetration further down-market. Patent protection, while historically strong, is time-limited and being challenged.

## Adjacent gap
The clearest wedge is **mid-market and SMB fulfillment** — companies with 5,000–50,000 SKUs, 10,000–50,000 sq ft of warehouse space, and capex budgets under $500K that cannot afford an AutoStore system or the SI markup. AutoStore's channel model and minimum viable grid size structurally exclude this segment. A startup offering a modular, right-sized cube-storage or dense ASRS product with a RaaS/subscription pricing model and self-install or light-touch deployment could own the 50th-to-90th-percentile warehouse operator — a segment AutoStore has no current path to serve.
