# Bear Robotics
**Vertical:** Hospitality / service robotics | **URL:** https://www.bearrobotics.ai | **HQ:** Redwood City, CA | **Founded:** 2017 | **Stage/Funding:** Majority-acquired by LG Electronics (Jan 2025, ~$600M valuation); total raised ~$175M across 5 rounds including a $60M Series C (Mar 2024)

## Product
Bear Robotics makes front-of-house autonomous mobile robots (AMRs) for food service. Three SKUs: Servi (base food-delivery robot), Servi Mini (compact variant for tighter spaces), and Servi+ (multi-tray carrier, up to 4 large trays). All units run on an AI navigation stack with a cloud-based fleet management platform that operators use to monitor and coordinate multiple robots remotely. The robots handle food delivery from kitchen pass to table, and return dirty dishes — they do not cook, take orders, or operate in the kitchen.

## ICP
Mid-to-large casual dining and chain restaurant operators facing chronic front-of-house labor shortages. Primary geographies: US, Japan, South Korea, Singapore. Enterprise accounts dominate — named customers include Denny's Japan (200+ locations), Yakiniku King, Chili's, and Google campus cafeterias. Deal sizes not publicly disclosed; the model is B2B direct with RaaS subscription gaining share.

## Traction
Several thousand Servi units deployed across the US, Japan, South Korea, and Singapore as of 2024 (NRN, 2021; demand described as 10x pandemic-era baseline). Revenue figures not disclosed. LG's $60M investment in March 2024 and subsequent majority acquisition at ~$600M implied valuation (Korean press, Jan 2025) signal institutional confidence in unit economics, though the deal also gives LG a robotics platform asset, not just a pure revenue play.

## Tech moat
Moderate. Navigation AI and multi-robot fleet management software are the core IP. LG integration adds manufacturing scale and distribution reach via CLOi commercial robotics. Moat is thin on hardware (contract-manufactured); stickiness comes from operator workflow integration and fleet software switching costs. No meaningful regulatory or data-network moat identified publicly.

## Distribution model
Direct B2B sales to restaurant operators; SoftBank Robotics partnership used for Japan market distribution and mass production. RaaS subscription model growing alongside outright purchase. Post-LG acquisition, CLOi channel opens potential OEM/enterprise reseller paths in commercial real estate and hospitality beyond restaurants.

## Critical weakness
Extreme vertical concentration: nearly all revenue comes from casual dining chains. Hospitality robot sales fell 11% globally in 2024 (IFR 2025 World Robotics report), and chain restaurant capex is highly cyclical. LG's majority ownership also introduces strategic misalignment risk — Bear's roadmap may be subordinated to LG's broader CLOi ambitions rather than optimized for restaurant operators.

## Adjacent gap
Bear ignores non-restaurant hospitality entirely: hotels (room-service delivery, linen transport, amenity runs), senior-living facilities, and hospital patient-floor logistics share the same labor-shortage pain and similar physical environments but have different compliance needs, buying centers, and contract structures that Bear's restaurant-focused sales motion and product packaging do not address. A startup targeting hotel and senior-care AMR delivery with purpose-built compliance features (HIPAA-adjacent data handling for senior care, property-management-system integrations for hotels) could build a defensible niche before Bear/LG pivots its enterprise motion toward those segments.
