# Apptronik
**Vertical:** General-purpose humanoids | **URL:** https://apptronik.com | **HQ:** Austin, TX | **Founded:** 2016 | **Stage/Funding:** Series A (~$935M total raised; $5B+ valuation as of Feb 2026)

## Product
Apollo is a 5'8", 160 lb general-purpose humanoid robot with a 55 lb payload, 4-hour hot-swappable battery runtime (~22 hours/day continuous), 71 degrees of freedom, and a modular base that supports stationary, wheeled, or legged configurations. It targets repetitive labor in warehouses and manufacturing — trailer unloading, case picking, palletization, machine tending, and workcell delivery. A second robot platform is expected to be unveiled in 2026. AI capabilities are powered by Google DeepMind's Gemini Robotics models.

## ICP
Large enterprise manufacturers and 3PL operators in North America: automotive OEMs (Mercedes-Benz), electronics contract manufacturers (Jabil), and large logistics providers (GXO). Deal motion is enterprise direct. Geography is primarily US at commercial scale, with Japan Post Capital participation suggesting near-term Japan expansion. No mid-market or SMB motion is public.

## Traction
Pilot deployments with Mercedes-Benz (automotive assembly, since March 2024), GXO Logistics (proof-of-concept), and Jabil (electronics manufacturing, announced Feb 2025). Revenue figures are not public; company describes "significant customer demand" and is scaling manufacturing to fulfill orders. Pricing is reportedly comparable to a new car (~$50K) for purchase, with a parallel RaaS subscription option. Sources: GlobeNewswire Feb 2025, TechCrunch Feb 2026, robozaps.com.

## Tech moat
Google DeepMind partnership provides access to Gemini Robotics foundation models, which is a meaningful AI integration advantage over teams training from scratch. Hot-swappable battery and modular mobility platform are differentiated hardware design choices. Eleven years of hardware iteration from NASA/DARPA-era origins at UT Austin. Moat is moderate — hardware specs are replicable; the AI model dependency on Google is a double-edged lock-in.

## Distribution model
Direct enterprise sales with two commercial tracks: outright purchase (~$50K/unit) and RaaS subscription. No channel or OEM partnerships disclosed. Sales motion is white-glove pilot-to-expansion with large named accounts.

## Critical weakness
Customer concentration and pilot-to-scale conversion risk: all disclosed deployments are early-stage pilots with a small number of marquee logos. If Mercedes or GXO do not expand beyond proof-of-concept, the revenue ramp needed to justify a $5B valuation stalls. Additionally, full dependency on Google DeepMind for AI models means Apptronik's software differentiation is not fully proprietary — a Google strategic shift or competing licensee could erode the moat quickly.

## Adjacent gap
The entire disclosed ICP is Fortune 500 manufacturers and large 3PLs in North America — minimum viable deal size and integration complexity lock out mid-market industrial operators (100–2,000 employees), regional 3PLs, and non-US geographies where labor arbitrage is tighter (Southeast Asia, Eastern Europe). A credible wedge: a lighter, cheaper humanoid or a managed-deployment RaaS layer targeting mid-market food & beverage or consumer packaged goods facilities that cannot absorb a $500K+ enterprise integration project. Alternatively, elder-care and healthcare ADL assistance — cited by Apptronik as a future vertical but completely unaddressed commercially — represents an emotionally differentiated market with willingness to pay and regulatory runway before any competitor locks in.
