# Coco Robotics
**Vertical:** Last-mile / sidewalk / road delivery | **URL:** https://www.cocodelivery.com | **HQ:** Santa Monica / Venice Beach, CA | **Founded:** 2019 (operational 2020) | **Stage/Funding:** Series B — $123M total ($80M Series B, June 2025); investors include Sam Altman, Max Altman, Pelion, Outlander, Offline Ventures, DeepWater

## Product
Coco operates a fleet of wheeled sidewalk delivery robots (90L cargo capacity, ~15-min delivery window, 1–2 mile radius) deployed in urban commercial corridors. Merchants opt in via a RaaS subscription or per-delivery fee routed through third-party platforms (DoorDash, Uber Eats). A single human teleoperator manages multiple robots simultaneously via a video-game-style interface, with sub-300ms failover. Robots charge overnight at merchant locations. The company also sells ad inventory on robot chassis.

## ICP
Quick-service and fast-casual restaurant chains in dense urban US metros (LA, Austin, Houston, Chicago). Key named partners: Subway, Wingstop, Jack in the Box, Shake Shack, Umami Burger. Deal structure is B2B2C — platforms pay per-delivery fees, larger enterprise chains pay RaaS subscriptions for dedicated fleets. Geography is US-first; Helsinki is an early EU proof-of-concept.

## Traction
500,000+ deliveries since 2020; 1M miles traveled; ~1,000 robots produced; 3,000 merchant partners (as of April 2026, per company website). No public revenue figure. Uber Eats LA and Miami integrations live; DoorDash enabling 600 merchants across LA and Chicago as of late 2024/early 2025. Sources: cocodelivery.com (April 2026), TechCrunch (June 2025), Restaurant Dive (2025).

## Tech moat
Perception stack (cameras + LiDAR), proprietary teleop interface, and a growing real-world urban navigation dataset ("millions of miles"). OpenAI partnership provides compute/AI access in exchange for real-world training data — potential future licensing of AI stack to third-party manufacturers. Physical AI lab launched Oct 2025 with UCLA Chief AI Scientist Bolei Zhou. Moat is moderate: hardware is simplified by design (lower cost, but also easier to copy), and navigation tech is commoditizing.

## Distribution model
B2B2C RaaS — direct sales to enterprise restaurant chains for dedicated fleets, plus marketplace integration via DoorDash and Uber Eats for long-tail merchant coverage. Sales motion is partnership-led; delivery platforms act as channel partners that bring merchant supply. Ad revenue is a secondary line.

## Critical weakness
Heavy platform dependency: a material share of revenue flows through DoorDash and Uber Eats, both of whom have strategic incentives to develop or acquire competing capabilities (Serve Robotics is partially DoorDash-aligned). Regulatory fragmentation also forces city-by-city permitting, capping expansion speed. If either major platform deprioritizes Coco or shifts volume to an owned solution, unit economics deteriorate quickly.

## Adjacent gap
Coco is fully optimized for warm-food, short-radius, restaurant-to-consumer delivery in high-density US coastal metros. The credible wedge is **temperature-controlled or multi-category last-mile in mid-density Tier-2/3 cities** (think: pharmacy, grocery, convenience) where sidewalk robot regulations are less contested, restaurant density is lower (reducing Coco's platform advantage), and no operator has locked in exclusivity. A narrower sub-wedge: **B2B campus or mixed-use district delivery** (corporate parks, university campuses, hospital campuses) where a single property manager can grant operating rights across the whole catchment area, eliminating the city-permit bottleneck that slows Coco's expansion.
