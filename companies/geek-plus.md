# Geek+
**Vertical:** Warehouse / fulfillment automation | **URL:** https://www.geekplus.com | **HQ:** Beijing, China | **Founded:** 2015 | **Stage/Funding:** Public — listed on HKEX July 2025 (stock code 2590.HK); ~10+ prior venture rounds totalling ~¥3B (~$415M); post-Series E1 valuation ¥15B (~$2.1B); market cap at IPO ~$2.8B

## Product
Geek+ designs, manufactures, and deploys autonomous mobile robots (AMRs) and supporting software for warehouse and fulfillment operations. Core hardware lines span goods-to-person shelf-carrying robots (P-series), tote-to-person (RS/RoboShuttle), pallet-handling (X/M-series), and sorting robots (S/F-series). These are sold or rented alongside a proprietary software stack — WES (warehouse execution), RMS (robot management), and an integration/orchestration platform (IOP) — capable of coordinating 5,000+ robots in a single facility.

## ICP
Large enterprise shippers, retailers, e-commerce operators, and third-party logistics (3PL) providers globally. Named customers include DHL, Walmart, Nike, and Decathlon. Deals are typically multi-robot deployments at established distribution centres; no public floor on order size, but the motion is enterprise-scale (hundreds to thousands of units per site). Geography skews toward China (historically) but 70% of 2024 revenue was international, with dedicated regional teams in the US, Europe (Germany, UK), Japan, Singapore, and South Korea.

## Traction
2024 full-year revenue: ¥2.41B (~$330M); 2021–2024 CAGR 45%. H1 2025 revenue: ¥1.025B (+31% YoY), gross margin 35.1%, adjusted EBITDA turned positive. 56,000+ robots deployed across 800+ enterprise customers in 40+ countries. Ranked #1 globally by AMR revenue for seven consecutive years (Interact Analysis, 2025). Customer repurchase rate ~74.6% (2024). Sources: Robotics & Automation News Sep 2025; 36kr IPO note Jul 2025.

## Tech moat
Multi-robot scheduling algorithms (proven at 5,000+ robot scale) represent genuine engineering depth. Breadth of hardware SKUs (goods-, tote-, and pallet-to-person plus sorting in one portfolio) reduces integration fragmentation for large customers. Manufacturing scale in China drives unit-cost advantages. QR-code + SLAM navigation is industry-standard, not proprietary. Software platform is moderately sticky post-deployment but not locked-in. Moat is real but primarily one of scale and operational track record, not hard IP.

## Distribution model
Primarily direct enterprise sales with regional teams owning large accounts. Supplemented by a certified-integrator channel for last-mile installation, testing, and training (e.g., Systemex Automation in North America). RaaS/rental option available (launched explicitly in Japan; offered selectively elsewhere) to lower capex barrier. Sales motion is consultative and project-based — long cycles, large deal sizes.

## Critical weakness
China-headquartered manufacturing creates acute tariff exposure in the US and increasingly in Europe. Any escalation in trade policy (US Section 301 tariffs, EU supply-chain legislation) directly compresses margins or prices Geek+ out of North American deals. Secondary weakness: enterprise-only motion leaves the mid-market and SMB segment structurally unaddressed — minimum viable deployments are too large and sales cycles too long for operators with 1–5 facilities.

## Adjacent gap
The clearest wedge is **mid-market and SMB fulfilment operators** (50–500 employees, 1–3 DCs) in the US and Western Europe who need AMR automation but cannot absorb a 6–18 month enterprise sales cycle, a six-figure capex commitment, or a dedicated integration team. Geek+'s motion, pricing, and channel are calibrated for DHL-scale relationships; it does not serve a regional 3PL, a growing DTC brand, or a speciality retailer with two warehouses. A competitor offering a turnkey RaaS product — small fleet, fast deployment, self-serve onboarding, locally manufactured to sidestep tariffs — could own this segment before Geek+ restructures its go-to-market downmarket.
