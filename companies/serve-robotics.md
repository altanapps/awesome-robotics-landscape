# Serve Robotics
**Vertical:** Last-mile / sidewalk / road delivery | **URL:** https://www.serverobotics.com | **HQ:** Redwood City, CA | **Founded:** 2021 (spun out of Uber) | **Stage/Funding:** Public (Nasdaq: SERV); ~$247M raised total; $86M post-IPO round closed Jan 2025

## Product
Serve deploys autonomous, electrically-powered sidewalk delivery robots (Gen3 "Saymo") under a Robotics-as-a-Service model. Restaurants and retailers do not buy the hardware; instead, Serve operates the fleet and charges per delivery through platform integrations with Uber Eats and DoorDash. The robot carries ~13 gallons of cargo, travels at up to ~11 mph, and runs on Nvidia Jetson Orin compute for onboard perception and navigation.

## ICP
Primary payer is the delivery platform (Uber Eats is the anchor contract, covering up to 2,000 robots; DoorDash added in 2025). End merchants — QSR chains and fast-casual (Shake Shack, Little Caesars, White Castle, Jersey Mike's) and convenience (7-Eleven) — are the demand source but not the direct billing relationship. Geography is dense urban U.S.: 6 metro areas, 20 cities as of end-2025. Deal structure is per-delivery RaaS; full utilization of the Uber Eats contract is projected at $60–80M annualized run-rate revenue.

## Traction
- FY 2025 revenue: $2.7M (above $2.5M guidance); Q4 fleet revenue +159% YoY
- Fleet: 2,000 robots deployed across 20 U.S. cities by Dec 2025 (20x growth in 2025)
- 1.8M+ deliveries completed to date; 99.8% completion rate
- 4,500+ merchant partners
- 2026 revenue guidance raised to ~$26M
- Sources: Serve Robotics Q4 2025 earnings (Jan 2026), GlobeNewswire Jan 2025

## Tech moat
Proprietary urban-environment dataset accumulated across millions of sidewalk miles, feeding a physical-AI flywheel (perception, navigation, multi-robot coordination). Patents cover sidewalk navigation and multi-robot coordination. Nvidia Jetson Orin hardware integration gives compute headroom. Hospital-corridor data from the Diligent Robotics acquisition cross-trains indoor navigation. Moat is real but early — the dataset advantage compounds with fleet size, but the fleet is still small versus the addressable environment.

## Distribution model
RaaS via delivery platform partnerships (Uber Eats, DoorDash). No direct sales to merchants. Platform integrations funnel order demand; Serve earns a per-delivery fee. Capital-intensive: Serve owns and operates the hardware, self-funding capex (~$25M projected for 2026) to avoid equipment financing costs.

## Critical weakness
Single-platform revenue concentration: the Uber Eats contract is the bulk of existing and projected 2026 revenue. Any renegotiation, exclusivity reversal, or platform-side pivot to an in-house solution (cf. Amazon Scout precedent) would be existential. The company is also deeply unprofitable at $2.7M revenue against a market cap that was ~$700M at peak — sustained dilution risk is high. Geographic density requirement limits addressable cities to a narrow set of walkable, permitting-friendly metros.

## Adjacent gap
Serve is locked into U.S. food-delivery platforms and dense QSR corridors in six metros. The unserved wedge is **last-mile in mid-density suburban or campus environments** — university campuses, hospital campuses, business parks, and gated communities — where sidewalk infrastructure exists but order density is too thin for Serve's platform-mediated model. A startup could attack this with a managed-fleet or white-label RaaS sold directly to campus operators or municipalities, bypassing the Uber Eats dependency entirely. International markets (UK, EU cities with permissive micro-mobility regulation) are a second uncontested geography; Serve has no announced non-U.S. plans.
