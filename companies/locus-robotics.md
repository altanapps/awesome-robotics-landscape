# Locus Robotics
**Vertical:** Warehouse / fulfillment automation | **URL:** https://locusrobotics.com | **HQ:** Wilmington, MA | **Founded:** 2014 | **Stage/Funding:** Series F — $416M raised; $2B post-money valuation (Nov 2022); investors include G2 Venture Partners, Goldman Sachs, Tiger Global, BOND, Prologis Ventures

## Product
Locus sells a fleet of autonomous mobile robots (AMRs) for warehouse picking, putaway, induction, and replenishment, delivered primarily as Robots-as-a-Service (RaaS). Three hardware SKUs: Locus Origin (collaborative picker alongside associates), Locus Vector (transport/material handling), and Locus Array (fully autonomous fulfillment, launched April 2026). All fleets are coordinated by LocusONE, a real-time orchestration layer that manages mixed human-robot workflows.

## ICP
Large 3PLs (DHL Supply Chain, CEVA Logistics, Kenco), big-box retail and e-commerce fulfillment operators, healthcare distributors (Cardinal Health), and industrial/beauty/apparel wholesalers. Predominantly North America and EMEA, with some APAC presence. Deal size is enterprise; typical deployments span multiple sites.

## Traction
6 billion cumulative picks as of Oct 2025, with the last billion completed in 24 weeks — the fastest pace on record (BusinessWire, Oct 2025). 150+ customers across 350+ sites; tens of thousands of robots deployed. 30–40% year-over-year volume growth in 2025; ~45M picks/week, targeting 60M/week at Q4 peak. Revenue reported at ~$230M in 2023 (Latka); no audited 2024–2025 figure publicly available.

## Tech moat
Operational data from billions of real picks feeds LocusONE's coordination algorithms — a meaningful data flywheel. RaaS contract structure creates switching costs and recurring revenue. Limited hardware IP moat; AMR components are commoditising. No manufacturing scale advantage comparable to Kiva/Amazon. Regulatory moat: none.

## Distribution model
Direct enterprise sales with a RaaS subscription model. Customers pay per-robot or per-pick rather than a large capex purchase. Channel partnerships with 3PLs serve as indirect distribution. No marketplace or OEM model disclosed.

## Critical weakness
Locus is heavily exposed to e-commerce volume cycles — it acknowledged overcounting post-pandemic demand, conducted layoffs in early 2024, and restructured sales/marketing. The RaaS model defers revenue and requires deep capital to fund robot fleets on customers' behalf, making the business burn-intensive at scale. Competitors (6 River Systems/Shopify, Berkshire Grey, Boston Dynamics) are closing the feature gap, and Amazon's internal AMR build-out pressures 3PL customers to in-source.

## Adjacent gap
Locus is almost entirely focused on large, multi-site enterprise 3PLs and Tier-1 retailers — deployments requiring hundreds of robots and multi-year contracts. **The unserved wedge is mid-market fulfillment operators (50–500 staff, 1–3 sites) in the UK and EU** who cannot absorb enterprise RaaS minimums or long implementation timelines. A lighter-weight, faster-to-deploy AMR offering priced at a lower per-robot floor — or a pay-per-pick model with sub-4-week go-live — could capture this segment before Locus or Geek+ moves downmarket.
