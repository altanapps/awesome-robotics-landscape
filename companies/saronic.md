# Saronic Technologies
**Vertical:** Defense / dual-use robotics | **URL:** https://www.saronic.com | **HQ:** Austin, TX | **Founded:** 2022 | **Stage/Funding:** Series D — $1.75B raised Mar 2026 at $9.25B valuation; prior rounds include $600M Series C (Feb 2025). Total disclosed capital ~$2.4B+.

## Product
Saronic designs and manufactures AI-enabled unmanned surface vessels (USVs) ranging from 6 to 150 feet, sold to the U.S. military and allied navies. Named platforms include Spyglass, Cutlass, Corsair, Cipher, Mirage, and the 150-foot Marauder MUSV. All vessels run a unified autonomy software stack called "Echelon," which handles mission planning, navigation, and sensor fusion in GPS/comms-denied environments. The company is vertically integrated: it owns shipyards in Austin, TX and Franklin, LA (the latter undergoing a $300M expansion), targeting production of 20+ vessels per year by 2027 and 600/year at full capacity.

## ICP
Primary customer is the U.S. Navy / NAVSEA via Other Transaction Authority (OTA) contracts — i.e., large government procurement outside traditional FAR rules. Secondary customers include U.S. Special Operations Command and the Defense Innovation Unit. Stated expansion targets: UK, Australia, Japan, South Korea, Middle East allied navies. Deal sizes are large-program government contracts ($8M–$392M range per agreement). Coast Guard and domestic law enforcement are named future channels but not yet contracted.

## Traction
Estimated 2024 revenue: $12.5M; estimated 2025 revenue: $200M (~1,500% YoY). NAVSEA $392M OTA production contract through May 2031 (initial award ~$197M). Prior DIU prototype OTA ($26M+) and SOCOM agreement ($8M+). Corsair platform moved from prototype to high-rate production in under one year. Source: Sacra equity research, March 2026; DefenseScoop, August 2025.

## Tech moat
Echelon software stack (unified autonomy across all vessel sizes) is the primary IP claim; manufacturing vertical integration and existing NAVSEA OTA relationship create switching cost and procurement lock-in. Regulatory moat is real — OTA vehicles and export-control compliance take years to establish. Data moat from operational deployments is plausible but not publicly substantiated.

## Distribution model
Direct government sales (prime contractor model). Sales motion is relationship-driven procurement through NAVSEA, DIU, and SOCOM program offices. International expansion is via allied-nation government-to-government or direct FMS-adjacent channels. No channel partners or marketplace model. Vigor Marine Group announced as a strategic manufacturing partner.

## Critical weakness
Single-customer concentration: near-total revenue dependence on U.S. Navy continuation of its "hybrid fleet" strategy. A policy shift, budget sequestration, or program restructure could cut demand significantly. Secondary risk: production execution — scaling a shipyard to 600 vessels/year ahead of confirmed multi-year orders is capital-intensive and operationally unproven at this scale.

## Adjacent gap
Saronic's entire motion is optimized for large-platform, high-dollar U.S. Navy programs — leaving the sub-$5M, shorter-lead-time segment underserved. Specifically: allied-nation coast guards and maritime law enforcement agencies in Southeast Asia and the Pacific Islands that need low-cost, easy-to-operate ISR and interdiction USVs but cannot access U.S. Navy OTA procurement pipelines, do not qualify for FMS priority, and lack the political weight to be Saronic's next port call. A startup purpose-built for this tier — simpler vessels, lighter autonomy stack, local-partner distribution, sub-sovereign sales motion — could own a geography Saronic structurally ignores.
