# Tesla Optimus
**Vertical:** General-purpose humanoids | **URL:** https://www.tesla.com/AI | **HQ:** Austin, TX (Gigafactory Texas) | **Founded:** 2021 (Optimus program) | **Stage/Funding:** Public (TSLA); Optimus CapEx embedded in Tesla's $20B+ 2026 CapEx guidance — no separate raise

## Product
Tesla Optimus is a 5'8", ~57 kg bipedal humanoid robot currently in pre-commercial production. Gen 2 was the demo vehicle; Gen 3 entered limited production at Tesla's Fremont factory in early 2026. The robot is designed for unstructured general labor — pick-and-place, parts kitting, quality inspection, and battery cell sorting — initially inside Tesla's own factories. Target retail price is ~$20,000–$30,000 at volume; early enterprise units are estimated at $100,000–$150,000 in low-volume runs. No commercial SKU has shipped to external buyers as of April 2026.

## ICP
No external customers as of Q1 2026. Internal target is Tesla's own manufacturing lines. Stated future ICP is large industrial manufacturers and logistics operators who need flexible, dexterous labor at scale — primarily US and potentially China (Shanghai Gigafactory being evaluated for Optimus production). Deal size at volume would be fleet contracts; no pricing structure for external buyers has been published.

## Traction
Zero verified external deployments as of Q1 2026. Elon Musk confirmed on the Q4 2025 earnings call that no Optimus units are doing "useful work" — they are in data-collection and learning mode. Over 1,000 Gen 3 units are reported deployed inside Tesla facilities for R&D. Volume production at Fremont is targeted to start July–August 2026 at an unspecified rate. A dedicated 10-million-unit/year factory at Gigafactory Texas was announced in November 2025, with a 2027 target. (Sources: Electrek, April 2026; Tesla Q4 2025 earnings)

## Tech moat
Real moat: (1) FSD/Autopilot-derived vision and sensor-fusion stack repurposed for robot locomotion and manipulation — years of real-world edge-case data. (2) Vertical integration across actuators, battery packs, and compute (Dojo/HW4 silicon). (3) Gigascale manufacturing infrastructure already financed and partially built. Thin moat: dexterous manipulation is still unproven in production; 10,000 unique parts creates serious supply-chain fragility, exposed by current Chinese rare-earth export restrictions on actuator magnets.

## Distribution model
Direct sales (no channel announced). Internal deployment first, then direct enterprise contracts. No RaaS pricing published. Sales motion is likely top-down, CEO-to-CEO for initial enterprise logos given the product is not yet commercially available.

## Critical weakness
Zero revenue-generating external deployments after five years of development and repeated missed production targets. Supply-chain concentration risk (Chinese rare-earth magnets for actuators) is an active constraint. The product exists primarily in demo and R&D mode; every competitor with a shipping robot (Agility Digit with Amazon, Unitree G1 at $13,500) is accumulating real-world operational data and customer trust that Tesla does not yet have. Political risk around Musk's public profile also creates enterprise buyer hesitation.

## Adjacent gap
Tesla's entire go-to-market is oriented toward Tier 1 industrial manufacturers — large, slow-moving procurement cycles, internal-first deployment, and a $100K+ per-unit early price point. This leaves a wide-open wedge for a startup targeting **mid-market warehouse and 3PL operators** (50–500 employees, North America or UK) who cannot wait for Tesla's production ramp, cannot afford $100K+ per unit, and need a robot that integrates with existing WMS software today. Agility Robotics partially occupies this space but is still enterprise-focused and Amazon-captive. A purpose-built, narrower-task humanoid (or near-humanoid) sold as RaaS at sub-$3K/month with WMS connectors and a 30-day pilot program could own this segment before Tesla's volume production makes it irrelevant — likely a 24–36 month window.
