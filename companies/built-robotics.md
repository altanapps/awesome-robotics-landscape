# Built Robotics
**Vertical:** Construction robotics | **URL:** https://www.builtrobotics.com | **HQ:** San Francisco, CA | **Founded:** 2016 | **Stage/Funding:** Series C — $112M total (Tiger Global, NEA, Founders Fund, Building Ventures, Next47)

## Product
Built Robotics sells two hardware-plus-software products. The **Exosystem** (2021) is an aftermarket autonomy kit — GPS, cameras, and AI — that bolts onto existing excavators and heavy civil equipment to enable fully autonomous operation, managed via their cloud platform "Everest." The **RPD 35** (2023) is a purpose-built robotic pile driver targeting utility-scale solar installations. Both products enable equipment to run autonomously on jobsite tasks (grading, trenching, piling) with remote supervision rather than an on-machine operator.

## ICP
Large US general contractors and specialty subcontractors in energy infrastructure — specifically utility-scale solar and wind farms, and heavy civil. Named customers include Mortenson, Black & Veatch, MPC Kinetic, and Sunstate. Deployments are US-centric with some presence in Australia. Deal size is not publicly disclosed but points to enterprise-level contracts given the customer profile.

## Traction
No public revenue figure. Company claims to be the first to commercially deploy fully autonomous heavy equipment in construction. Deployments span multiple US states and Australia. September 2025 contract announced with Blattner (large solar EPC) for solar piling autonomy. Partnership with the International Union of Operating Engineers (400,000+ members). Headcount is 11–50 (Tracxn/Wikipedia, 2025).

## Tech moat
Proprietary sensor-fusion and autonomy stack tuned for unstructured outdoor terrain; multi-year head start on real-world deployment data in solar piling. The Everest cloud platform creates some switching cost via fleet management and jobsite data lock-in. IP filings exist but patent moat is unclear. Manufacturing moat is thin — Exosystem is a retrofit kit, not a ground-up machine.

## Distribution model
Direct sales to large ENR 400 contractors and EPCs. RaaS (Robotics-as-a-Service) model for deployment; hardware is provided as part of the service engagement rather than sold outright. Union partnership with IUOE is a credentialing/trust mechanism with labor, not a channel.

## Critical weakness
Revenue is entirely dependent on a small number of large solar EPC contracts — customer concentration risk is high. The retrofit Exosystem approach means Built is constrained to equipment types and jobsite conditions where third-party machines can be instrumented, limiting addressable precision. With only 11–50 employees and $112M raised through Series C, burn versus contract-based revenue is an open question; no follow-on round has been announced publicly as of April 2026.

## Adjacent gap
Built is laser-focused on utility-scale solar and heavy civil in North America, run by large-contractor buyers with long procurement cycles. The unserved wedge is **small-to-mid-size civil contractors** (site prep, municipal utilities, roadwork) in **Europe and Southeast Asia** who cannot afford or access enterprise RaaS contracts but face the same acute labor shortage. A lighter-weight, lower-cost autonomy retrofit — or a task-specific robot for a single high-frequency task like trenching — aimed at the $5M–$50M contractor tier, sold through equipment rental channels (Sunbelt, Herc) rather than direct, could capture a segment Built structurally ignores.
