# Canvas
**Vertical:** Construction robotics | **URL:** https://www.canvas.build | **HQ:** San Francisco, CA | **Founded:** 2017 | **Stage/Funding:** Series B ($24M raised, lead Menlo Ventures + Brick & Mortar Ventures, Apr 2021); core technology acquired by JLG Industries, Jan 2026 (terms undisclosed)

## Product
Canvas built a semi-autonomous drywall finishing robot — the 1200CX — that combines a mobile base (built on a JLG aerial platform), a robotic arm, proprietary end effectors, LiDAR-based room mapping, and computer-vision-trained spray/sand routines to deliver Level 4 and Level 5 drywall finishes. The commercial model was RaaS: $12,500/month with a six-month minimum, deployed by Canvas as a tech-enabled subcontractor rather than a sold unit. One human operator supervised multiple machines on site.

## ICP
Commercial general contractors and drywall subcontractors on large-footprint interior projects (multi-family residential, commercial office, hospitality) in the US. Deal size implied by the $12,500/month RaaS price and multi-month minimums suggests mid-to-large subcontractors with predictable high-volume finishing work. Geographic focus was US only; union cooperation (District Council 16, IUPAT) was part of the go-to-market.

## Traction
No public revenue figures disclosed. The 1200CX was commercially available for reservation by September 2024. JLG noted a six-year prior relationship with Canvas (i.e., since ~2020), implying extended pilot/deployment history, but no customer count or deployment numbers were published. Series B ($24M) was the last disclosed funding event before the JLG acquisition.

## Tech moat
Proprietary end effectors for spray and sand operations, LiDAR-driven autonomous room segmentation, and ML-trained vision for surface quality. Team pedigree (Boston Dynamics, SRI, Apple, Otherlab, MIT) was a hiring moat. Moat was moderate — the core autonomy stack was not patented publicly at scale, and the JLG acquisition suggests the technology value was more in integration knowhow than hard IP.

## Distribution model
RaaS (Robot-as-a-Service) sold direct as a subcontractor engagement — Canvas crews brought the machines on-site and ran them. No channel or OEM resale before the JLG acquisition; JLG now holds the technology and will likely integrate it into its own equipment/rental business.

## Critical weakness
The subcontractor model created a capital-intensity and margin problem: Canvas owned and operated expensive hardware, bore maintenance costs, and had to staff each deployment. This constrained scaling and likely drove the asset sale to JLG rather than a standalone exit. The semi-autonomous requirement (human operator per cluster) also capped labor savings at a level that didn't fully close the ROI case for smaller contractors.

## Adjacent gap
Canvas was US-only, RaaS-only, and limited to interior drywall finishing on large commercial jobs. The unserved wedge is **small-to-mid residential contractors in markets with acute finishing labor shortages** (UK, Australia, parts of Europe) where union constraints are lighter, job counts are high but per-job square footage is lower, and no RaaS incumbent exists. A capital-lighter model — selling or leasing the machine outright with a software/data subscription rather than running it as a crew — could serve this ICP at lower CAC and without the operational overhead that killed Canvas's unit economics.
