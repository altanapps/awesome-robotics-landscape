# BRINC Drones
**Vertical:** Defense / dual-use robotics | **URL:** https://www.brincdrones.com | **HQ:** Seattle, WA, USA | **Founded:** 2017 | **Stage/Funding:** Series C — $157.2M total raised ($75M Series C in Apr 2025, led by Index Ventures; Motorola Solutions strategic investor)

## Product
BRINC sells a suite of purpose-built public-safety drones and supporting infrastructure: the **Lemur 2** (indoor tactical drone with glass-breaking capability and two-way comms), the **Responder** and **Guardian** (outdoor drones in two size classes), paired **Stations** (autonomous launch/charging docks), the **BRINC Ball** (throwable communicator), and **LiveOps** (SaaS platform for teleoperation, livestreaming, evidence management, and CAD/dispatch integrations). Hardware is manufactured in the USA. A **Safeguard** subscription bundles unlimited repairs, replacements, and software upgrades into recurring revenue.

## ICP
US municipal and county public-safety agencies — law enforcement (patrol, SWAT), fire departments, and search-and-rescue units. Currently deployed across 900+ agencies in all 50 US states; claims 10%+ of US SWAT teams. Deal size is not publicly disclosed; sales likely run $50K–$500K per agency depending on fleet size and software tier, with Safeguard adding ARR. Geography is overwhelmingly domestic USA.

## Traction
900+ public-safety agency deployments across all 50 US states as of 2025 (company-stated). 1-in-3 calls reportedly cleared by drone alone; average on-scene arrival under 70 seconds. Revenue undisclosed. Valuation was $300M in 2023; post-Series C valuation not disclosed. (Sources: TechCrunch Apr 2025, BRINC website Apr 2025.)

## Tech moat
Domestic manufacturing provides NDAA compliance — a hard regulatory gate that locks out DJI and most Chinese hardware for US government contracts. LiveOps creates switching costs via evidence-chain integrations and CAD tie-ins. Operational data from 900+ agencies fuels autonomy and dispatch-optimization models. Purpose-built form factors (glass-break, window entry, throwable comms) are difficult to replicate with general-purpose platforms. Patent portfolio extent is not public.

## Distribution model
Direct enterprise sales to public-safety agencies, supplemented by the Motorola Solutions channel partnership (Apr 2025) which plugs BRINC into Motorola's existing CAD/dispatch and body-camera sales motions across thousands of agencies. Grant assistance page on site suggests they support agencies in accessing federal DHS/COPS funding. Recurring revenue via Safeguard subscription.

## Critical weakness
Near-total US-government dependency means growth ceiling is bounded by domestic agency budgets and procurement cycles. Motorola partnership de-risks distribution but also creates channel lock-in risk. The company has no visible international commercial traction — EU and allied-nation public-safety markets remain unaddressed — and a valuation ($300M+) that demands significant revenue scale to justify, leaving it vulnerable if US federal grant flows slow or a Chinese-drone ban is reversed/delayed.

## Adjacent gap
BRINC is purpose-built for US municipal public safety and has made no visible move into international allied-nation markets (UK, Australia, Canada, Germany) where DJI bans are also advancing and domestic-manufacture requirements are emerging. A startup targeting **NATO-allied municipal first-responders** — particularly in the UK, where the Home Office has active DfT/NPAS drone programmes and no dominant US-made alternative — could attack the same DFR (drone-as-first-responder) wedge with NDAA-equivalent compliance positioning, without competing head-to-head with BRINC's Motorola channel in the US. Secondary gap: mid-market fire and EMS agencies priced out of BRINC's full stack who need a single-SKU outdoor responder drone with minimal integration burden.
