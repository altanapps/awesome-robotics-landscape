# Moon Surgical
**Vertical:** Surgical / medical robotics | **URL:** https://moonsurgical.com | **HQ:** Paris, France (with US presence in San Francisco, CA) | **Founded:** 2019–2020 | **Stage/Funding:** Series B — ~$92M total raised (Series A $31.3M, 2022; Series B $55.4M, 2023, co-led by Nvidia and JJDC)

## Product
Moon Surgical makes the Maestro Robotic Surgery System — a two-armed robotic assistant that attaches to a standard laparoscopic OR setup and holds or manipulates tissue under surgeon control, replacing the human surgical assistant role. It is not a full autonomous surgical robot; it augments the lead surgeon's hands rather than replacing them. The 2024 commercial FDA-cleared version added ScoPilot (AI-guided camera control) and Maestro Insights (cloud analytics). A 2025 update added 5G/Wi-Fi connectivity enabling continuous cloud-based AI feature delivery.

## ICP
General surgeons and laparoscopic specialists at hospitals and ambulatory surgery centers (ASCs) in the US and EU performing soft-tissue procedures (cholecystectomy, hernia, colorectal). Primary buyer is hospital/ASC capital procurement. Geographic focus is US and Western Europe. Deal size not publicly disclosed; capital equipment sale or lease assumed given device-class model.

## Traction
Over 2,300 patients treated as of early 2026 (per MedTech Dive, 2025–2026). Limited Market Release through 2024; broader commercial launch targeted for 2025. No revenue figures disclosed. Customer count not public. (Source: MedTech Dive, 2026; PR Newswire, 2024.)

## Tech moat
FDA 510(k) clearance (twice cleared: Dec 2022 and June 2024) and CE mark create meaningful regulatory barriers. Nvidia co-investment and cloud AI roadmap (ScoPilot, Maestro Insights) suggest a data flywheel strategy as procedures accumulate. Board chaired by Intuitive co-founder Frederic Moll adds distribution credibility. Hardware moat is modest — the assistant-robot category is nascent but not locked up by patents Moon exclusively owns.

## Distribution model
Direct sales to hospitals and ASCs in the US and Europe. Sales motion appears capital-equipment-led (device plus software platform). No channel or OEM partnerships announced publicly. Limited Market Release phasing suggests a high-touch, reference-site-driven sales motion typical of early surgical robotics.

## Critical weakness
At 2,300 procedures across an unknown number of sites, commercial traction is thin relative to $92M raised. The assistant-robot category depends on surgeons accepting a workflow change without the full autonomy payoff — value proposition is narrower than a full robotic system (Intuitive, CMR Surgical) and requires co-selling the ROI of replacing a human scrub assistant. If hospitals find the incremental benefit insufficient versus existing assistant staffing, adoption stalls.

## Adjacent gap
Moon targets well-resourced Western hospitals for elective soft-tissue laparoscopy. The unserved wedge is **mid-volume hospitals and ASCs in emerging markets (Southeast Asia, Middle East, Latam)** where OR assistant shortages are acute, infrastructure for full robotic suites (da Vinci, Hugo) is prohibitive, and the laparoscopic base is large and growing. A lower-cost, modular assistant robot priced for markets where a $1.5M+ full system is inaccessible — and where surgeon-to-assistant ratios are worse — could capture significant volume that Moon's pricing and sales motion will not reach.
