# ABB Robotics
**Vertical:** Industrial arms / cobots | **URL:** https://new.abb.com/products/robotics | **HQ:** Zürich, Switzerland | **Founded:** 1988 (robotics unit; ABB Group 1988 via ASEA/BBC merger) | **Stage/Funding:** Public (NYSE: ABBNY); Robotics division being sold to SoftBank Group for $5.4B (deal announced Oct 2025, close expected 2026)

## Product
ABB Robotics sells industrial robots (IRB series, 6-axis articulated arms across a wide payload/reach range), collaborative robots (YuMi dual-arm, GoFa single-arm up to 12 kg, SWIFTI high-speed cobot), and autonomous mobile robots (AMRs via the Autonomous Mobile Robots portfolio). The offer extends to robotic systems integration, the RobotStudio simulation/programming software, Wizard Easy Programming for code-free setup, field services, spare parts, and digital subscriptions. Hardware is the revenue core; software and services are growing but secondary.

## ICP
Large manufacturers in automotive, electronics, metalworking, food & beverage, and logistics — predominantly in Europe, North America, and China. Deal sizes range from single-unit integrator purchases (~$25K–$35K for cobots) to multi-million-dollar turnkey lines sold to Tier-1 OEMs and large factories. Primary buyers are engineering/automation managers at companies with 500+ employees; SMEs are an aspirational but underpenetrated segment.

## Traction
$2.3B revenue for the Robotics division in 2024/2025 (Rockingrobots, 2025; ABB Integrated Report). Operational EBITA margin ~12.1%. Holds the global #2 position by robotics revenue (behind Fanuc). Exact installed-base unit count not publicly broken out.

## Tech moat
Long IP portfolio in motion control and path planning built over 35+ years. RobotStudio is a mature simulation environment with deep integration into ABB hardware. Manufacturing scale and a global spare-parts/service network create switching costs for existing customers. Regulatory certifications (CE, UL, ISO 10218) across every major market are a meaningful barrier for new entrants. These moats are wide but aging — not AI-native.

## Distribution model
Predominantly indirect: a global network of Value Providers (system integrators, distributors, machine builders) who configure and resell ABB robots into end-customer lines. ABB sells direct to strategic automotive/electronics OEMs. No marketplace or RaaS model at scale. Sales motion is enterprise B2B with long cycles (3–18 months for large lines); cobot unit sales are shorter but still integrator-mediated.

## Critical weakness
ABB was structurally late to cobots (YuMi 2015, GoFa/SWIFTI not until 2021) and has underinvested in AI-native programming and fleet intelligence. The SoftBank acquisition signals that ABB itself concluded the division needed an AI partner to remain competitive. The integrator-only go-to-market means ABB has almost no direct relationship with the end-user, making it blind to usage data and slow to iterate on software. Pricing ($25K–$35K for cobots) is exposed to Chinese competitors (e.g., Doosan, Elephant Robotics) pricing 30–50% lower.

## Adjacent gap
ABB's channel model and enterprise minimum viable deal size leaves SME manufacturers (10–200 employees) in Europe and North America almost entirely unserved — these buyers lack the in-house engineering to work with integrators, cannot justify $30K+ upfront, and need turnkey vertical applications (welding, pick-and-place, palletizing) with zero-code setup and a RaaS or subscription pricing model. A startup that bundles a mid-payload cobot (or white-labels one) with an AI-driven application layer, vertical-specific onboarding, and outcome-based pricing could land in the gap ABB's distribution structure structurally cannot fill.
