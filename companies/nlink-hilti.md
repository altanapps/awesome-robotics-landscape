# nLink (Hilti)
**Vertical:** Construction robotics | **URL:** https://www.hilti.com | **HQ:** Sogndal, Norway (R&D) / Schaan, Liechtenstein (Hilti parent) | **Founded:** nLink founded ~2013; Hilti strategic investment (37% stake) August 2017 | **Stage/Funding:** Subsidiary/strategic partnership — Hilti took a 37% stake in 2017; financial terms undisclosed; nLink remains partially independent as the R&D arm

## Product
The flagship product is the Jaibot, launched October 2020 — a semi-autonomous, cordless, BIM-enabled mobile ceiling-drilling robot targeting MEP (mechanical, electrical, plumbing) installations. The robot uses the Hilti PLT 300 robotic total station for indoor positioning, reads drilling plans generated from BIM models via Hilti's cloud service, and drills overhead anchor holes with integrated dust control. It operates at heights of 2.65–5 m, achieves up to 90 holes per hour, runs ~8 hours per charge, and marks completed holes by trade. A 2022 update added capability for corrugated metal deck ceilings and concrete walls. No additional robots from this team have been publicly announced.

## ICP
MEP subcontractors and large general contractors managing MEP scopes on mid-to-large commercial or mixed-use construction projects. Primary early adopters include Tier 1 contractors such as Skanska, Bouygues, YIT, and De Groot. Geography is global via Hilti's distribution network, with documented deployments in Finland, continental Europe, and North America. Deal size is not publicly disclosed; Hilti's standard model suggests equipment rental/lease rather than outright sale.

## Traction
Jaibot has been deployed on multiple sites by Skanska, Bouygues, YIT, and De Groot as of 2022–2023. YIT's Tuultenristi project in Espoo, Finland (2022–2023) is the most detailed public case study — reported 30% productivity gain on drilling and measurement. Exact fleet size or total deployment count is not public. Described by industry analysts as "arguably the most successful construction robot to date" in 2024 context. (Sources: AEC Business 2022; Gerard de Valence Substack 2024.)

## Tech moat
Three interlocking layers: (1) Hilti's PLT 300 total station provides a proprietary indoor positioning reference that ties Jaibot into Hilti's broader jobsite digitization ecosystem; (2) BIM-to-drill-plan cloud workflow creates switching costs once a contractor's BIM workflows are integrated; (3) Hilti's global direct sales force and ON!Track asset management platform give distribution coverage no pure-play robotics startup can replicate. The nLink IP (positioning algorithms, ceiling-robot kinematics) is embedded in the Hilti stack.

## Distribution model
Hilti direct sales — the same enterprise sales force that sells power tools and anchoring systems. Robots are offered as a managed service/rental package bundled with Hilti engineering support (MEP design teams generate drilling plans from customer BIM files). This is effectively RaaS wrapped inside Hilti's existing fleet management and service infrastructure, sold motion-by-motion into existing contractor accounts.

## Critical weakness
The Jaibot requires structured BIM input and relatively predictable ceiling conditions — it cannot adapt in real time to unplanned site changes, spatial constraints, or non-standard ceiling types. Worker supervisors must manually reposition the robot outside its 2-meter auto-radius. This makes it unsuitable for smaller projects, renovation/retrofit work, or sites without mature BIM workflows. The product is also locked to Hilti's ecosystem; contractors not already using Hilti tools or ON!Track face adoption friction.

## Adjacent gap
The clearest wedge is **retrofit and renovation drilling for MEP upgrades in existing commercial buildings** — a segment Jaibot explicitly cannot serve because it requires BIM data that older buildings lack and tolerates messy, non-repetitive ceiling conditions. The ICP is MEP subcontractors performing building upgrades (HVAC retrofits, electrical rework) rather than new construction, concentrated in markets with large existing commercial stock undergoing energy retrofits (UK, Germany, US). A robot designed for low-BIM, adaptive overhead work — with onboard sensing to map-and-drill rather than plan-and-drill — could own a segment Hilti cannot enter without rebuilding its core workflow assumption.
