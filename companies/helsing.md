# Helsing
**Vertical:** Defense / dual-use robotics | **URL:** https://helsing.ai | **HQ:** Munich, Germany | **Founded:** March 2021 | **Stage/Funding:** Series D — €1.37B total raised; ~€12B valuation (June 2025)

## Product
Helsing sells AI software and autonomous hardware to European defense ministries. Software products include Altra (reconnaissance-strike targeting), Cirra (electronic warfare AI, integrated into the Eurofighter's Arexis EW suite via Saab), and Centaur (autonomous fighter-pilot AI). Hardware products include HF-1 and HX-2 loitering munitions (GPS-independent navigation using stored map data) and the SG-1 Fathom autonomous underwater surveillance drone. The CA-1 Europa — an 11m, 4-tonne unmanned combat aircraft — is in development with a first-flight target of 2027. In 2025 Helsing acquired Grob Aircraft to bring manufacturing in-house.

## ICP
European NATO-member defense ministries and prime contractors (Saab, Airbus, Rheinmetall). Deal sizes range from low-eight figures (software integration) to framework contracts exceeding €1B (Bundeswehr HX-2 loitering munitions, February 2026). Ukraine is an active wartime customer (4,000 HF-1 delivered, 6,000 HX-2 contracted).

## Traction
Bundeswehr awarded an initial €269M HX-2 contract in February 2026, with a framework option up to €1B. Saab–Helsing signed a "three-digit million Euro" Cirra integration contract in November 2025. Ukraine deliveries represent the most public operational deployment at scale. Revenue not publicly disclosed; 2021 filings showed €415K — not meaningful given the 2024–2026 contract ramp. (Sources: Bloomberg, PitchBook, Wikipedia, 2025–2026.)

## Tech moat
Primary moat is data from live wartime deployments in Ukraine — real sensor and targeting feedback that no lab-only competitor can replicate. Secondary moat is regulatory and trust: Helsing is purpose-built as a European sovereign alternative to US defense tech (Anduril, Palantir), which gives it access to procurement channels closed to non-EU vendors. The Grob acquisition adds manufacturing leverage but is early. IP on GPS-denied navigation and the Cirra EW stack is defensible in the short term; long-term durability depends on whether wartime data advantages compound.

## Distribution model
Direct government sales to defense ministries via long-cycle procurement processes, supplemented by prime-contractor partnerships (Saab, Airbus) that embed Helsing software into existing platform programs. No marketplace or channel model. Sales motion is relationship-driven, ministry-by-ministry, with multi-year framework contracts.

## Critical weakness
Bloomberg reported in April 2025 that former employees, investors, and military experts raised concerns about overpriced drones and glitchy software reliability. The Rheinmetall partnership dissolved in 2024. Deeper structurally: Helsing is dependent on a small number of very large government contracts, each subject to parliamentary budget approval (the Bundestag capped the HX-2 framework at €1B per producer). A single program cancellation or political shift in defense spending could crater near-term revenue. The gap between marketing claims and operational reality in complex battlefield environments is also a growing area of external scrutiny.

## Adjacent gap
Helsing is almost entirely focused on Tier-1 European NATO militaries with nine-figure procurement budgets and multi-year procurement cycles. **The unserved wedge is smaller NATO and EU member states** — Poland, the Baltic states, Romania, the Nordic non-Helsing markets — that have urgent force-modernization needs, lack domestic defense tech ecosystems, and cannot navigate the minimum deal sizes and timelines that Helsing requires. A startup offering modular, lower-cost AI targeting or ISR software designed for rapid fielding on off-the-shelf drone hardware (rather than bespoke platforms) could own this segment before Helsing's procurement machinery reaches it.
