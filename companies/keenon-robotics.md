# Keenon Robotics
**Vertical:** Hospitality / service robotics | **URL:** https://www.keenon.com | **HQ:** Shanghai, China | **Founded:** 2010 | **Stage/Funding:** Series D — $228.8M total raised (led by SoftBank Vision Fund 2, CICC ALPHA, Aramco Ventures Prosperity7; Series D closed Sep 2021 at $200M)

## Product
Keenon designs and manufactures autonomous mobile robots (AMRs) for commercial service environments across three core lines: DINERBOT (T3/T8/T9/T9 Pro/T10/T11 — multi-tray food and beverage delivery for restaurants and hotels), BUTLERBOT (W3 — multi-floor hotel room-service delivery with elevator integration and compartmentalized hygienic storage), and KLEENBOT (C20/C40/C55 — commercial floor cleaning). Navigation stack combines laser SLAM, visual SLAM, UWB, lidar, and encoders. Hardware is manufactured in-house in China. In March 2025 the company also unveiled the XMAN-R1 humanoid platform alongside new KLEENBOT models, signaling an attempt to move up the stack.

## ICP
Primary buyers are chain operators in hospitality (hotel groups, restaurant chains, KTV/entertainment venues) and healthcare (hospitals, nursing homes). Secondary markets include retail malls and office buildings. Core revenue is China-first — over 60% domestic catering-robot market share as of 2022 — with international presence in the US, Europe, South Korea, and Singapore handled via regional resellers and distributors. Deal sizes are typically per-unit hardware purchases; lease/RaaS structures exist but are not the dominant model.

## Traction
$139M revenue in 2025 (CBInsights estimate); $14.4M in 2019, indicating rapid scaling through the 2020–2024 period. Dominant domestic position: 60.4% share in China's catering-robot market (2022). Notable early customer: Haidilao (intelligent restaurant pilot, 2018). International footprint is live but publicly unquantified in unit terms. Sources: CBInsights (2025 revenue), Crunchbase/Robot Report (funding), ZoomInfo/Tracxn (market share).

## Tech moat
Moderate. Keenon's moat rests on: (1) manufacturing scale and vertical integration in China, yielding cost advantages Western peers cannot easily replicate; (2) a large proprietary SLAM + navigation dataset built across thousands of real-world commercial deployments; (3) Haidilao and other chain-operator relationships that create switching costs via fleet-management software integrations. IP portfolio exists but is not regarded as uniquely defensible. No meaningful regulatory moat. Network effects are weak — robots operate independently per venue.

## Distribution model
Primarily direct hardware sales to enterprise chains in China; international markets served through a reseller/channel-partner model (regional distributors in the US, Europe, South Korea, Singapore). Some RaaS pilots exist but hardware sales dominate reported revenue. Sales motion is B2B enterprise — account managers targeting chain procurement teams. No meaningful marketplace or OEM presence disclosed.

## Critical weakness
Deep China-revenue concentration makes Keenon vulnerable to geopolitical hardware restrictions and tariffs (US, EU) that raise landed costs for international buyers. The channel-partner model in Western markets creates a thin, fragmented footprint with no direct customer feedback loop, making iteration slow. Reliance on one-time hardware sales without a sticky SaaS layer means recurring revenue is low and churn (robot replacement cycles) is the retention mechanism. Humanoid pivot (XMAN-R1) risks diluting focus before the core AMR business reaches true global scale.

## Adjacent gap
Keenon's channel model leaves **independent and small-chain restaurant operators in English-speaking markets** almost entirely unaddressed — their resellers target large enterprise procurement, and per-unit prices ($15K–$25K+ CAPEX) are prohibitive without financing. A credible wedge: a RaaS-native, software-first delivery-robot service priced at $500–$800/month targeting 2–20 location independent operators in the US or UK, paired with a lightweight integration layer for existing POS systems (Toast, Square). Keenon will not descend into this segment because the ACV is too low for their enterprise sales motion and it conflicts with their channel partners.
