# Covariant
**Vertical:** Foundation models for robotics | **URL:** https://covariant.ai | **HQ:** Emeryville, CA | **Founded:** 2017 | **Stage/Funding:** Series C — $222M total raised (Index Ventures, Radical Ventures, Canada Pension Plan, Gates Frontier); August 2024 Amazon reverse-acquihire: $380M non-exclusive license + ~25% of staff hired, founders departed

## Product
Covariant sells AI software — the Covariant Brain and its successor foundation model RFM-1 — that runs on third-party robotic arms (ABB, Knapp, etc.) to perform warehouse manipulation tasks: bin picking, case picking, order sortation, item induction, goods-to-person picking, kitting, and depalletization. Customers license the software stack and integrate it with their own or a system-integrator-supplied hardware. RFM-1 (launched March 2024) is a multimodal foundation model trained on text, images, video, robot actions, and numerical sensor data gathered from 30+ robot arm variants since 2018.

## ICP
3PLs, e-commerce fulfillment operators, and pharmaceutical distributors in North America and Europe running high-SKU-count distribution centers. Named customers include McKesson (pharma), Otto Group (e-commerce/DE), Radial (3PL/US), ABB (SI/OEM channel). Deal sizes are enterprise — multi-year software licenses bundled through system-integrator channel partners.

## Traction
Dozens of customers globally as of 2023; 6x revenue growth in 2022 (source: Covariant Series C press release, April 2023). No ARR figure publicly disclosed. RFM-1 announced March 2024. Post-Amazon deal, Covariant continues operating under new CEO Ted Stinson (former COO); a 2025 whistleblower complaint described the entity as a "zombie company" (GeekWire, 2024).

## Tech moat
Proprietary robotics training dataset accumulated from 30+ arm variants and billions of real-world manipulation events since 2018 — this data flywheel is the primary defensible asset. The RFM-1 model architecture is not open-sourced. The Amazon non-exclusive license, however, means the core IP is now accessible to Amazon and potentially diluted competitively.

## Distribution model
Indirect/channel: Covariant sells through industrial robotics OEMs and system integrators (ABB, Knapp) who bundle the software into turnkey fulfillment installations. Sales motion is enterprise — long cycles, integrator-mediated. No self-serve or marketplace layer.

## Critical weakness
The Amazon deal structurally hollowed Covariant out: founding team gone, core IP licensed non-exclusively to the world's largest warehouse operator, and ~25% of staff departed. The remaining company must sell against a well-funded ex-employer who holds the same IP. The channel-only model also limits direct customer relationships and pricing power.

## Adjacent gap
Covariant is exclusively focused on large North American and European 3PL/e-commerce DCs — enterprise deals, high integration overhead, SI-mediated. The unserved wedge is **mid-market manufacturers and regional 3PLs** (50–500 employees, single-facility) in **Southeast Asia and LATAM** that need flexible manipulation AI but cannot afford or access ABB/Knapp integration costs. A startup offering a lighter-weight, hardware-agnostic picking AI with a self-install or thin-SI model — targeting $50K–$150K annual deals rather than $500K+ — could build a foothold Covariant's channel economics make structurally unattractive for them to chase.
