# Distalmotion
**Vertical:** Surgical / medical robotics | **URL:** https://www.distalmotion.com | **HQ:** Epalinges, Switzerland | **Founded:** 2012 | **Stage/Funding:** Series G — $150M raised Nov 2025 (led by Revival Healthcare Capital; strategic investor: Johnson & Johnson Innovation)

## Product
Distalmotion sells the DEXTER Robotic Surgery System, a soft-tissue surgical robot cleared by the FDA for gallbladder removal, hysterectomy, and hernia repair. DEXTER is positioned as a hybrid laparoscopic-robotic platform: it operates with conventional laparoscopic vision systems, requires roughly 400 sq ft of OR floor space, and lists at approximately $800K — roughly half the footprint and cost of a da Vinci Xi. A pay-per-procedure financing model supplements outright purchase to reduce upfront commitment for smaller facilities.

## ICP
Independent and health-system-affiliated Ambulatory Surgery Centers (ASCs) in the United States performing high-volume general surgery and gynecology cases. Deal size is ~$800K capital purchase or pay-per-procedure arrangement. European hospital OPDs are a secondary market; the $150M Series G is explicitly earmarked for US ASC commercialization.

## Traction
Approximately 3,000 patients treated globally as of late 2025 (Globenewswire, Nov 2025). First US ASC acquisition announced March 2025 (Northtowns ASC). Revenue not publicly disclosed. European clinical use predates US clearance. Scale is early-commercial, not yet at meaningful installed-base volume.

## Tech moat
Regulatory clearance (three FDA 510(k) indications) is a real near-term barrier. The hybrid architecture — compatibility with existing laparoscopic towers rather than requiring a proprietary vision stack — lowers switching cost for the facility but creates a defensibility question long-term. IP around the wristed instrument mechanics and the compact console provides some protection. No meaningful data network effect yet given low procedure volume.

## Distribution model
Direct sales in the US, targeting ASC administrators and surgeons simultaneously. European sales through a direct or thin-channel model from Swiss HQ. J&J Innovation's strategic stake may open a distribution or co-commercialization pathway but no formal OEM/channel agreement is publicly confirmed.

## Critical weakness
Reimbursement in ASCs is structurally lower than hospital outpatient departments — CMS rates for the same robotic procedure can be a fraction of HOPD rates. This compresses the economic case for ASC operators to justify an $800K capital purchase or incremental per-procedure fee. Intuitive is actively addressing ASCs by redeploying older da Vinci Xi systems at discounted terms, and has the installed-base surgeon relationships Distalmotion lacks. With only ~3,000 total procedures globally, Distalmotion has limited outcomes data to use in procurement conversations.

## Adjacent gap
The clearest wedge is international ASC and day-surgery markets that Distalmotion is not yet funding: UK, Germany, and the Gulf (UAE/Saudi), where private pay-per-procedure day-surgery is growing fast and Intuitive penetration is thin outside major academic centers. A startup or competitor that pairs a similarly compact robot (or a robotics-as-a-service model built on a licensed platform) with local reimbursement expertise and a regional sales motion could capture facilities that can't afford or don't qualify for da Vinci but aren't yet in Distalmotion's US-first commercial pipeline.
