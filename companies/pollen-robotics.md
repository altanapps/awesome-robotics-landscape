# Pollen Robotics (Hugging Face)
**Vertical:** Picks-and-shovels | **URL:** https://www.pollen-robotics.com | **HQ:** Bordeaux, France | **Founded:** 2016 | **Stage/Funding:** Acquired by Hugging Face, April 2025 (undisclosed); prior VC: €2.5M (Bpifrance); Hugging Face has raised $395M+ total

## Product
Pollen Robotics sells Reachy 2, a full-body open-source humanoid robot ($70,000) designed for embodied AI research and teleoperation. Hardware uses a proprietary 7-DOF Orbita joint system; software stack is Apache 2.0 licensed and integrates with Hugging Face's LeRobot library. Post-acquisition they launched Reachy Mini, a desktop-format version starting at $299 (lite) / $449 (wireless), co-manufactured with Seeed Studio and aimed at education and prototyping. All CAD, firmware, and models are publicly available on the Hugging Face Hub.

## ICP
Research institutions and university labs (Cornell, Carnegie Mellon, CNRS, CEA, Ecole Polytechnique) globally; enterprise AI/robotics teams (Accenture) running embodied-AI pilots; and, with Reachy Mini, individual developers and educators. Deal size ranges from ~$300 (Reachy Mini) to $70,000 (Reachy 2). Primary geography is North America and Europe; 20+ countries reached.

## Traction
100+ Reachy 2 units deployed across 20+ countries (Hugging Face acquisition blog, April 2025). Reachy Mini shipped 3,000 units as of early 2026 (Seeed Studio, January 2026). LeRobot GitHub library: 12,000+ stars within first year of launch. Revenue undisclosed.

## Tech moat
Open-source community lock-in via LeRobot (models, datasets, tutorials on HF Hub); Orbita joint IP; 9 years of hardware iteration; NVIDIA GR00T N1 integration partnership; and Hugging Face's platform distribution to ~1M ML developers. ANA Avatar XPRIZE 2nd place (2022) validates teleoperation capability.

## Distribution model
Direct sales via email for Reachy 2 (high-touch, enterprise/lab motion). Reachy Mini sold through Seeed Studio's hardware distribution network and Hugging Face's own storefront — lower-touch, volume-oriented. Open-source community drives organic pull; documentation, tutorials, and model weights on HF Hub funnel researchers toward hardware purchase.

## Critical weakness
Fully open hardware means any contract manufacturer can clone or undercut the physical product without license risk. Pollen's moat depends on community and software ecosystem stickiness rather than hardware exclusivity — a better-funded player (e.g., Unitree, or a Seeed Studio competitor) can commoditize the form factor once reference designs are public. The $70,000 Reachy 2 price tag also gates out the majority of university labs that lack NSF-scale budgets.

## Adjacent gap
Reachy 2 targets well-funded Western research labs; Reachy Mini targets individual hobbyists. Neither targets the **mid-market institutional buyer**: community colleges, vocational-training programs, and mid-size corporate L&D teams in Southeast Asia and Latin America who need a sub-$5,000 programmable robot with curriculum support and local-language documentation. A startup could own this segment by reselling or rebranding Reachy Mini hardware with a managed SaaS curriculum layer and regional support — a picks-and-shovels play on top of an already open picks-and-shovels platform.
