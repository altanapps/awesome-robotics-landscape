# Aigen
**Vertical:** Agricultural robotics | **URL:** https://www.aigen.io | **HQ:** Redmond, WA | **Founded:** 2020 | **Stage/Funding:** Series A — $19M total ($4M seed 2022, $12M Series A Nov 2023, led by ReGen Ventures with NEA, Cleveland Avenue, Incite, Susquehanna)

## Product
Aigen sells Element, a solar-powered autonomous weeding robot deployed in fleets ("crews") that roam row-crop fields, identify weeds via onboard AI vision (stereo depth perception), and deliver precision mechanical strikes — no herbicides. The gen2 (launched April 2025) adds 50% more solar capacity, 4x faster AI compute, and all-wheel drive. Delivered as a robotics-as-a-service (RaaS) subscription; robots operate autonomously via mesh network, with agronomic data fed back to the farmer. Orders for the 2026 season are open; no public price-per-acre disclosed.

## ICP
Large-scale US row-crop farmers facing herbicide-resistant weed pressure and farm-labor shortages. Primary crops: sugar beets (Red River Valley, ND), cotton (California Central Valley via Bowles Farming), and soy. Early customers appear to be mid-to-large family farms (Bowles is a sixth-generation, multi-thousand-acre operation). Geography is US-only for now, with stated ambitions to expand globally.

## Traction
2025: ~50 units operational, 30 actively running across three farms (source: search aggregation, April 2025). 2024 target was 20,000+ acres; 2024 pre-orders reportedly sold out in one day. Manufacturing capacity rated at 3,000 robots/year from Redmond facility. Revenue not disclosed. Customer count not public.

## Tech moat
Proprietary weed-strike AI trained on row-crop imagery accumulated since 2020 — data flywheel deepens with each season deployed. Solar-primary energy eliminates battery logistics that constrain competitors. Gen2 compute upgrade (4x) suggests rapid iteration. Engineering team draws from SpaceX and Amazon Robotics. Moat is moderate: IP and training data are real, but the underlying vision + strike approach is replicable with capital.

## Distribution model
Direct RaaS — Aigen retains robot ownership, deploys fleets seasonally, and charges farmers a service fee. Sales motion appears to be founder-led direct outreach to large farms, with partnership announcements (e.g., Bowles) used as proof points for next-season order cycles.

## Critical weakness
Crop and climate specificity: current robots are validated for sugar beets, cotton, and soy in flat, dry US geographies. Scaling to new crops, row geometries, or wetter climates requires retraining data and hardware re-validation — each new crop is effectively a new product launch. With only ~50 units deployed in 2025, there is a wide gap between stated manufacturing capacity (3,000/yr) and actual field presence, creating execution and cash risk before unit economics are proven at scale.

## Adjacent gap
Aigen is anchored in large US row-crop farms (500+ acre operations, dry western/midwestern geographies) and entirely absent from specialty crops — berries, tree nuts, vegetables — where weed pressure and chemical-reduction pressure are equally acute, labor costs are higher, and farmers often have stronger willingness to pay per acre. California's specialty-crop growers (strawberries, almonds, leafy greens) face the same herbicide-resistance dynamics with no RaaS weeding option today. A startup targeting specialty-crop weeding with a robot designed for irregular canopy geometries and varied row spacing could own that segment before Aigen's roadmap reaches it.
