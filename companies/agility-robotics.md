# Agility Robotics
**Vertical:** General-purpose humanoids | **URL:** https://www.agilityrobotics.com | **HQ:** Salem, OR | **Founded:** 2015 | **Stage/Funding:** Series C — $641M total raised; $400M Series C (March 2025) at ~$2.12B valuation. Investors: Amazon, DCVC, Playground Global, NVIDIA NVentures, Sony Innovation Fund.

## Product
Agility sells Digit, a bipedal humanoid robot designed for unstructured indoor environments — warehouses, fulfillment centers, and light manufacturing. Digit stands ~5'9", handles payloads up to 35 lbs (50 lb in next-gen), and performs repetitive tasks such as tote-moving, bin transfer, and pick-and-place. It is offered both as a capital purchase (~$150K/unit) and via a RaaS subscription (~$8–10K/month/unit) that bundles software, maintenance, and fleet orchestration through Agility's Arc cloud platform. Agility also operates RoboFab, a purpose-built manufacturing facility in Salem designed for modular, scalable Digit production.

## ICP
Large logistics operators and manufacturers with high labor turnover (30–40% annually) and existing facility layouts that preclude fixed automation. Current paying customers include Amazon, GXO Logistics, Spanx, and Toyota Motor Manufacturing Canada. Deals are enterprise B2B, direct sales, multi-year RaaS contracts. Geography is primarily North America.

## Traction
~100 Digit units deployed commercially as of early 2026, making Agility the only humanoid company with robots doing confirmed revenue-generating work at scale. GXO signed the industry's first formal humanoid RaaS commercial agreement; Toyota Motor Manufacturing Canada signed a commercial RaaS agreement after a successful pilot (announced February 2026). Revenue not publicly disclosed. (Sources: Sacra 2025, SiliconAngle/TechStartups April 2025, Morningstar February 2026.)

## Tech moat
Strongest moat is first-mover deployment data — ~100 real-world units generating operational telemetry competitors lack. Arc cloud platform creates fleet-level switching costs. RoboFab's modular ARMS assembly line provides manufacturing process IP. Bipedal form factor avoids facility retrofit costs, which is a real procurement advantage over fixed automation.

## Distribution model
Direct enterprise sales with multi-year RaaS contracts as the primary motion. No channel or OEM partnerships disclosed. Sales cycle is long (pilot → commercial agreement), anchored by a small number of strategic anchor customers.

## Critical weakness
Customer concentration is severe — Amazon is both a major investor and a top customer, and Amazon has its own internal robotics division (Proteus, Sparrow). If Amazon deprioritizes Digit in favor of in-house solutions, Agility loses its most visible reference customer and a funding anchor simultaneously. At ~100 units, Agility has not yet demonstrated manufacturing scale; unit economics at thousands of robots per year remain unproven.

## Adjacent gap
Agility's entire commercial footprint is large North American enterprises with long procurement cycles. The unserved wedge is **mid-market manufacturers and 3PL operators in Europe** (Germany, Netherlands, UK) — facilities with 50–500 workers, labor cost structures that justify humanoid ROI, but no vendor offering localized integration, CE-mark compliance, and a sub-$5M entry point. A startup that pairs a lower-cost humanoid platform (or resells a Chinese platform like Unitree with a Western compliance/services layer) and builds a European-first distribution motion could own that segment before Agility's direct sales org reaches it.
