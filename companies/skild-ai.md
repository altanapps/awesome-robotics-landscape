# Skild AI
**Vertical:** Foundation models for robotics | **URL:** https://www.skild.ai | **HQ:** Pittsburgh, PA (East Liberty); additional offices in San Francisco Bay Area and Bengaluru | **Founded:** 2023 | **Stage/Funding:** Series C — $1.4B raised (Jan 2026) at $14B+ valuation; led by SoftBank, with NVentures (NVIDIA), Bezos Expeditions, Macquarie Capital, Lightspeed, Sequoia, Coatue, Felicis, LG, Schneider Electric, Salesforce Ventures

## Product
Skild sells the **Skild Brain**, a unified robotics foundation model designed to control any robot body — quadrupeds, humanoids, tabletop arms, mobile manipulators — across any task without hardware-specific fine-tuning. The model trains on human internet video and physics-based simulation, enabling in-context adaptation to new environments and robot morphologies in real time. Commercial use cases include security/inspection, warehouse operations, last-mile delivery, factory assembly, data-center management, and construction. It is deployed as a software layer on top of OEM robot hardware rather than as a complete robot system.

## ICP
Large enterprise operators in logistics, manufacturing, security, and facilities management — primarily North American and likely expanding to Asia via LG/Samsung strategic investors. No disclosed deal sizes; revenue reaching ~$30M suggests mid-to-large enterprise contract sizes with multiple named customers not yet disclosed publicly.

## Traction
~$30M live revenue reached within a few months in 2025, described as "growing rapidly with multiple customers" across security, construction, delivery, data centers, warehouses, and assembly. Source: Skild AI Series C announcement, January 2026 (businesswire.com).

## Tech moat
Cross-morphology training data (human video + sim) is a legitimate data flywheel advantage over single-robot-type incumbents. In-context learning without retraining lowers deployment friction. Founders Deepak Pathak (CMU, ex-Berkeley) and Abhinav Gupta (ex-Meta AI) have strong academic IP lineage. Strategic investors LG, Samsung, and Schneider Electric could provide proprietary hardware and deployment data. Moat is currently research/talent-depth-based; model commoditization risk is real as open-source robotics foundation models mature.

## Distribution model
Direct enterprise sales with a software-licensing or RaaS-style motion layered on OEM hardware. Strategic investors (LG, Schneider Electric) double as channel partners and beta deployers. No marketplace or self-serve tier evident.

## Critical weakness
Revenue concentration risk at the early stage ($30M across unnamed "multiple" customers). The "omni-bodied" positioning, while compelling, requires customer-side robot hardware procurement — Skild does not control the hardware layer, creating dependency on OEM partners and slowing sales cycles. At a $14B valuation on $30M revenue, execution risk is extreme and the company must reach hundreds of millions in ARR to justify the cap table, forcing premature scaling before the model is fully proven in hard industrial environments.

## Adjacent gap
The Skild Brain targets large-enterprise, North American/Asian operators with complex multi-robot fleets. The unserved wedge is **SME-scale operators in Europe** — mid-market manufacturers, logistics providers, and facility managers (50–500 employees) who need a single-robot-type solution (e.g., mobile inspection only) at lower price points, with GDPR-compliant on-premise deployment. Skild's enterprise direct-sales motion, US-centric partnerships, and "omni-bodied" complexity are structural reasons they will not serve this segment in the near term. A focused, single-morphology foundation model (e.g., mobile manipulation for European food manufacturing) with on-prem deployment and a lighter onboarding motion is the credible wedge.
