# iRobot
**Vertical:** Domestic / consumer robotics | **URL:** https://www.irobot.com | **HQ:** Bedford, MA (retained post-restructuring) | **Founded:** 1990 | **Stage/Funding:** Private; acquired 100% by Shenzhen Picea Robotics via prepackaged Chapter 11 (emerged Jan 23, 2026); ~$254M in Picea claims converted to equity; prior NASDAQ-listed (IRBT)

## Product
iRobot sells the Roomba line of autonomous robot vacuums (entry 105-series through premium 705 Combo with AutoWash Dock) and the Braava line of robot mops. The 2025 lineup spans $200–$900 MSRP, with the premium and mid-tier segments ($300+) representing roughly 74% of robot revenue. A U.S.-based subsidiary, "iRobot Safe Corporation," was established post-emergence to govern domestic user data under a citizen-only board. The competing 3i vacuum line (Picea's own brand) was discontinued in February 2026 to consolidate under Roomba.

## ICP
Mass-market U.S. and European households willing to pay $300–$900 for hands-free floor cleaning; heaviest penetration in dual-income, mid-to-upper-income homes. North America and Europe are primary geographies. No meaningful enterprise or commercial-cleaning segment.

## Traction
Full-year 2025 trailing revenue approximately $570M (Q1: $101.6M, Q2: $127.6M, Q3: $145.8M — all down 24–33% YoY). Peak revenue was ~$1.43B in 2021. Cumulative devices sold: 20M+ globally. Market share estimated at 20–25% globally in 2024, but Roborock overtook iRobot as global revenue leader that year. Sources: Vacuum Wars Q3 2025 results; The Robot Report; MacroTrends IRBT revenue chart.

## Tech moat
1,300+ IP assets (patents covering navigation, mapping, docking). Twenty-plus years of consumer floor-mapping data and brand recognition ("Roomba" is genericized in U.S. households). Manufacturing cost advantage through vertical integration with Picea. Data firewall structure may offer regulatory differentiation in the U.S. vs. Chinese-owned rivals. Moat is moderate: competitors replicate SLAM navigation cheaply, and the brand premium has eroded as Roborock/Ecovacs close the quality gap at lower price points.

## Distribution model
Retail shelf (Best Buy, Target, Walmart, Costco) plus direct e-commerce (irobot.com) and Amazon marketplace. No RaaS or subscription hardware model. Sales motion is B2C retail pull, supported by digital advertising and seasonal promotions (Prime Day, Black Friday). International distribution via regional retail partners in Europe and APAC.

## Critical weakness
Three consecutive years of revenue decline (~60% peak-to-trough) wiped the balance sheet before Picea stepped in. Chinese private ownership under a manufacturing-focused parent creates integration risk, potential for R&D deprioritization, and reputational headwind in markets (U.S. government, enterprise) sensitive to data sovereignty — even with the Safe Corporation firewall. Talent retention in Bedford is uncertain under new ownership. The brand is strong but the product cycle has stagnated relative to Roborock's annual hardware cadence.

## Adjacent gap
iRobot has zero presence in the sub-$150 entry-level segment (ceded entirely to Eufy/Ecovacs) and no commercial or light-professional vertical (small offices, short-term rentals, co-working spaces) where scheduled, supervised cleaning has a clear ROI case. A credible wedge: a RaaS model targeting short-term rental operators (Airbnb hosts, boutique hotels) — a segment that needs daily floor cleaning, tolerates a monthly fee, and is underserved by consumer brands that sell hardware once and walk away. iRobot's brand is trusted but its model doesn't capture recurring revenue; a startup with a fleet-management layer and STR-operator distribution could own this niche before any incumbent notices.
