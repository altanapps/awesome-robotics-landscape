# Avride
**Vertical:** Last-mile / sidewalk / road delivery | **URL:** https://avride.ai | **HQ:** Newburyport, MA (offices in Austin, Tel Aviv, Belgrade, Seoul) | **Founded:** 2020 (spun out of Yandex SDG; majority-owned by Nebius Group) | **Stage/Funding:** Series E, up to $375M committed (Uber + Nebius, Oct 2024); ~270 employees

## Product
Avride sells two autonomous vehicle products sharing a common software stack: a sidewalk delivery robot (navigates at ~8 km/h, crosses roads via crosswalks) and a robotaxi built on retrofitted Hyundai Ioniq 5s with in-house lidar/radar/camera sensor suites. Robots are manufactured in Taiwan. Revenue model is robot-as-a-service, with Uber and Uber Eats as the primary distribution layer for both products.

## ICP
Food delivery platforms (Uber Eats, Grubhub) and ride-hailing networks (Uber) in dense urban cores and university campuses in the US, South Korea, and Japan. Deal structure is platform partnerships, not direct-to-merchant. Geography currently limited to Austin, Dallas, Jersey City (US), plus Rakuten/Japan campus pilots.

## Traction
250,000+ cumulative customer deliveries reported (Avride, 2025). ~100 sidewalk robots deployed at Ohio State University via Grubhub. 200 robotaxis in Dallas service as of April 2026. February 2025 Japan launch with Rakuten across Harumi district retail. Source: Wikipedia/Avride, TheRobotReport, TechCrunch (2025–2026).

## Tech moat
Proprietary AV software stack inherited from Yandex SDG (multi-year head start on sensor fusion and mapping). Shared "Universal Driver" architecture across sidewalk and road products gives cross-platform data compounding. Manufacturing relationship in Taiwan gives some hardware cost leverage. Moat is moderate — software lead is real but not insurmountable; hardware is outsourced.

## Distribution model
RaaS via exclusive platform partnerships (Uber/Uber Eats as primary channel, Grubhub for campus). Sales motion is top-down B2B2C: negotiate at platform level, deploy into their merchant/driver network. No direct-to-restaurant or direct-to-retailer sales motion visible.

## Critical weakness
Full dependency on Uber/Grubhub as distribution means Avride cannot set pricing, control merchant relationships, or expand without platform approval. If either platform shifts to a competing provider (Serve Robotics, Starship) or builds in-house, Avride loses its go-to-market. Public acceptance friction (vandalism in Philadelphia, regulatory backlash over sidewalk congestion) adds operational fragility. NHTSA logged 37 accidents in autonomous mode as of March 2026.

## Adjacent gap
Avride is entirely locked into food delivery via app-platform intermediaries and ignores the B2B/enterprise last-mile segment — think hospitals, corporate campuses, logistics hubs, and retail distribution centers that want to own or operate the robot fleet directly rather than route through a consumer app. A startup offering white-label or operator-owned sidewalk robots with open APIs (no Uber dependency), purpose-built for indoor-outdoor campus logistics (healthcare, manufacturing, retail), in geographies Avride has not entered (Europe, Southeast Asia, Middle East) could capture institutional buyers who cannot or will not route delivery through a consumer food-app stack.
