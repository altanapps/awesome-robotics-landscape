# Starship Technologies
**Vertical:** Last-mile / sidewalk / road delivery | **URL:** https://www.starship.xyz | **HQ:** Tallinn, Estonia (US ops: San Francisco, CA) | **Founded:** 2014 | **Stage/Funding:** Series C — $50M (Oct 2025); $280M+ total raised

## Product
Starship sells a Delivery-as-a-Service (DaaS) product built on a proprietary fleet of six-wheeled, sidewalk-navigating autonomous robots operating within a ~2-mile radius of a merchant. Retailers and delivery platforms integrate via API; end consumers order through partner apps (Grubhub, Bolt, DoorDash/Wolt, Foodora) or Starship's own app. The robot handles the last mile autonomously at Level 4; a remote operator pool provides exception handling. Fleet currently at 2,700+ robots; stated target is 12,000+ by 2027.

## ICP
Three segments: (1) university campuses — food and convenience retailers paying per-delivery fees on 60+ US campuses; (2) suburban grocery retailers in the UK and Europe across 30+ cities partnering with chains like Co-op; (3) corporate/industrial campuses for internal logistics. Deal structure is B2B (retailer or campus operator pays deployment/integration fee plus per-delivery revenue share). No public per-delivery pricing; estimated sub-$5 consumer delivery fee. Primary geography: US campuses, UK/Northern Europe suburban towns.

## Traction
9M+ autonomous deliveries completed as of October 2025; 2,700+ robots across 270+ locations in 7 countries; 125,000 road crossings per day. Company claims positive gross margins and profitability at the unit-economics level. Revenue figure not publicly disclosed. Source: Starship press release, October 2025; The Robot Report, 2024.

## Tech moat
Largest real-world sidewalk-autonomy dataset globally — 10M+ miles of operation feeds continuous model improvement. Regulatory approvals across 7 countries create meaningful re-entry cost for latecomers. Established OEM/platform integrations (Grubhub, Wolt) create switching-cost stickiness on the merchant side. Hardware unit economics improve at scale. IP moat is moderate; sensor stack (cameras + ultrasonic) is not exotic, and the moat is primarily data and regulatory incumbency, not patented hardware.

## Distribution model
Direct B2B sales to campus operators, grocery chains, and corporate facilities managers. Platform partnerships (delivery apps) act as demand-side aggregators. Model is RaaS (Robot-as-a-Service) with per-delivery monetization layered on. Sales motion is enterprise/institutional — long procurement cycles, facilities or procurement buyer, not self-serve.

## Critical weakness
Operational geography is structurally constrained: robots require flat, well-maintained sidewalks and low-pedestrian-density environments. Dense urban cores (New York, London central) and developing-market cities are effectively inaccessible. Campus and suburban-town deployments are high-trust but low-volume markets with a finite total addressable set. The entire model depends on regulatory goodwill — a single city-level ban or liability ruling can shut a market. Customer concentration risk is also high: losing one campus contract or one grocery chain partnership meaningfully impacts fleet utilization.

## Adjacent gap
Starship's enterprise-only sales motion and suburban/campus focus leaves the **SMB merchant in mid-density urban neighborhoods** entirely unserved. A startup could attack the wedge of independent restaurants, pharmacies, and convenience stores in walkable mid-size cities (think: Austin, Denver, Bristol, Rotterdam) that are too small for Starship's B2B sales cycle but dense enough to make per-robot economics work. A lighter-weight, API-first RaaS platform targeting these merchants — with a lower deployment minimum and a self-serve onboarding flow — would not compete head-on with Starship's institutional contracts but would capture the long tail of urban last-mile demand that Starship's model structurally ignores.
