# Berkshire Grey
**Vertical:** Warehouse / fulfillment automation | **URL:** https://www.berkshiregrey.com | **HQ:** Bedford, MA, USA | **Founded:** 2013 | **Stage/Funding:** Acquired by SoftBank Group (go-private, July 2023, ~$375M deal); prior SPAC at $2.7B valuation (2021); total raised ~$554M

## Product
Berkshire Grey sells a suite of five AI-powered robotic systems for warehouse and fulfillment operations: Core (robotic picking), Dispatch (parcel sorting), Scoop (trailer unloading), Stash (automated put wall for order consolidation), and Stride (shuttle-based high-density sorting). Systems are deployed as integrated solutions — not individual robots — targeting picking, sorting, packing, and unloading workflows in large-scale distribution centers.

## ICP
Large enterprise retailers, e-commerce operators, grocery chains, 3PLs, and parcel carriers in the US. Customers include Walmart, Target, FedEx, Maersk, TJX, and Bealls Florida. Deal sizes are enterprise-scale capital projects; sales cycle is long. Geography has been primarily North America, with some international exposure via Maersk and a CEVA Logistics project in Canada.

## Traction
Revenue estimated at ~$65.9M (FY2022, SEC 10-K) and ~$75M (2025 estimate, CB Insights). Operating losses were $164.3M on $50.9M revenue in FY2021. Customer count not publicly disclosed; known named accounts number roughly 5–10 logos. Nasdaq threatened delisting before SoftBank take-private in 2023.

## Tech moat
300+ patents in robotic perception, gripping, and planning. A decade of deployment data across unstructured warehouse environments gives some model advantage. SoftBank ownership provides balance-sheet depth. Moat is moderate — patents are defensible but narrow; competitors (GreyOrange, Covariant, Symbotic) are closing the perception gap.

## Distribution model
Direct enterprise sales with long consultative cycles. Deployed as turnkey integrated systems, not off-the-shelf products. No channel or OEM model evident. RaaS not prominently featured; primarily capex deals.

## Critical weakness
The company has never been profitable and burned through public investor capital before going private at a severe down-round valuation. SoftBank ownership stabilizes cash but removes market discipline. The direct, bespoke enterprise model does not scale unit-economically — each deployment is custom, limiting margin expansion and making it hard to move down-market.

## Adjacent gap
Berkshire Grey ignores mid-market warehouses (sub-500K sq ft, 100–500 employees) that cannot afford a multi-million-dollar bespoke integration project and lack the internal engineering teams to manage it. A credible wedge: a modular, plug-and-play robotic picking or sorting unit priced on a RaaS model targeting 3PLs and regional grocery distributors in the UK and Europe — geographies Berkshire Grey has not systematically entered — where labor cost pressure is acute and the enterprise incumbents have little installed base.
