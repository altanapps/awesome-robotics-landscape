# Figure AI
**Vertical:** General-purpose humanoids | **URL:** https://www.figure.ai | **HQ:** Sunnyvale, CA | **Founded:** 2022 | **Stage/Funding:** Series C — $1B+ raised (Sep 2025); $39B post-money valuation; ~$1.9B total raised. Lead: Parkway Venture Capital. Strategic investors: NVIDIA, Intel Capital, Brookfield Asset Management, Qualcomm Ventures, Salesforce, T-Mobile Ventures, LG Technology Ventures.

## Product
Figure sells general-purpose bipedal humanoid robots — currently Figure 02 (commercial) and Figure 03 (announced Oct 2025) — paired with Helix, a proprietary Vision-Language-Action (VLA) AI model. Helix runs a three-layer stack: a 7B-parameter VLA for high-level reasoning (System 2), a reactive policy at 200 Hz for motion translation (System 1), and a 1 kHz low-level balance/contact layer (System 0). Robots are sold or leased to enterprise customers for unstructured manual labor tasks in manufacturing and logistics. BotQ, Figure's own factory (launched Mar 2025), targets 12,000 units/year with a stated path to 100,000.

## ICP
Large-scale industrial manufacturers and logistics operators — initially automotive OEMs — in North America. First paying customer: BMW Group (Spartanburg, SC). Reported second customer: UPS. Deal size is undisclosed but structured as enterprise pilots with volume scale-up clauses. Geography is currently US-only; multinational expansion tied to BotQ capacity growth.

## Traction
Revenue-generating since Jan 2025. BMW deployment: Figure 02 robots ran 10-hour daily shifts over 11 months, loading 90,000+ parts across 1,250+ runtime hours, contributing to 30,000+ BMW X3 vehicles assembled (The Robot Report, 2025; Fortune, 2025). No public ARR figure. Unit economics and margin structure undisclosed.

## Tech moat
Vertical integration across hardware (in-house robot design), AI (Helix VLA trained on proprietary real-world deployment data), and manufacturing (BotQ). Real-world operational data from BMW is a compounding advantage — each deployment hour improves Helix. Patent portfolio and manufacturing tooling provide short-term barriers. Weakest moat layer: the underlying VLA architecture is reproducible; competitors with equivalent deployment data (Tesla, 1X) can close the gap.

## Distribution model
Direct enterprise sales to large manufacturers. Motion: founder-led BD, pilot-to-scale contract structure. No channel partners or marketplace currently. RaaS pricing model likely for volume deployments but not publicly confirmed.

## Critical weakness
Capital intensity without proven unit economics. At a $39B valuation and ~$1.9B raised, Figure must demonstrate gross-margin positive deployments before the next financing window. BMW scrutiny (Fortune, Apr 2025) flagged possible exaggeration of deployment scope — customer concentration in one pilot and perception risk from overstatement could stall enterprise sales cycles. Tesla's manufacturing moat and sub-$20K Optimus target price, if achieved, would commoditize the hardware layer and compress Figure's pricing power.

## Adjacent gap
Figure's ICP is large Western automotive OEMs running high-volume, highly repetitive line tasks — leaving mid-market manufacturers (500–5,000 employees), food & beverage processing, and Asian factory operators unserved. A credible wedge: a narrow-task humanoid or cobotic system targeting Japanese or South Korean mid-tier suppliers where labor shortages are acute, regulatory familiarity with robots is high, and OEM-level deal complexity is absent. Alternatively, the domestic/care-home segment (Figure 03 is aimed there but BotQ volume and enterprise focus make it a secondary priority) could be attacked by a leaner team building on open-source VLA foundations.
