# Miso Robotics
**Vertical:** Hospitality / service robotics | **URL:** https://misorobotics.com | **HQ:** Pasadena, CA | **Founded:** 2017 | **Stage/Funding:** Series E (Feb 2025); ~$65M raised to date via Reg A+ crowdfunding rounds and institutional; not publicly listed

## Product
Miso sells the Flippy Fry Station — an AI-guided robotic arm mounted at commercial deep-fryer stations that autonomously baskets, cooks, and transfers fried food items. It also acquired Zignyl (early 2026), an AI-powered restaurant-ops platform covering POS, labor scheduling, and payroll, positioning Flippy as one module inside a broader kitchen-management software suite. The Fry Station is the only SKU in active commercial deployment.

## ICP
Large quick-service restaurant (QSR) chains in the United States with high-volume fry stations and chronic hourly labor turnover — primarily franchise operators and corporate-run locations. Primary named customer is White Castle (350-location US chain); past pilots include Jack in the Box, CaliBurger, and Panera (both since ended). Deal size is approximately $5,000/month per location on a RaaS subscription.

## Traction
14 active Flippy units deployed as of end-2025 (White Castle and Insert Coin), down from 17 units two years prior. Net revenue was approximately $385K in 2024 (down from $493K in 2023). Sources: Fortune (Feb 2026), Kingscrowd (2024).

## Tech moat
Proprietary computer-vision models trained on live commercial kitchen data across multiple QSR chains — real grease, heat distortion, irregular food shapes. Some manufacturing know-how in form factor (Fry Station is roughly half the size of prior generation). Patent portfolio not independently verified as deep. Moat is thin relative to capital raised; data accumulation has stalled as deployments declined.

## Distribution model
Direct enterprise sales to QSR chain headquarters (corporate and franchise). RaaS model at ~$5K/month per unit. Miso handles installation, maintenance, and software updates. No channel partners or OEM relationships disclosed. Sales motion is top-down, corporate-to-franchisee mandate.

## Critical weakness
Revenue is effectively flat at under $500K/year after nearly a decade and $65M+ raised, with active unit count declining rather than growing. Lost partnerships with Panera and CaliBurger suggest the unit economics or operational integration are not compelling enough to sustain rollouts beyond pilots. The crowdfunding-heavy capital stack limits institutional credibility and may constrain future raise options. Cash flow is deeply negative relative to revenue.

## Adjacent gap
Miso is exclusively focused on frying in US fast-food chains — it does not serve ghost kitchens, stadium/event concessions, C&I cafeterias (corporate or hospital), or any non-US market. The most credible wedge is **high-volume institutional food service** (stadium concessions, university dining, hospital cafeterias) where labor scarcity is acute, menu is standardized, procurement cycles are structured, and operators have capital budgets rather than thin franchise margins. A competitor entering this segment avoids Miso's White Castle dependency, faces lower sales-cycle friction (fewer franchisee-vs.-corp dynamics), and can accumulate training data in a less contested environment.
