# Carbon Robotics
**Vertical:** Agricultural robotics | **URL:** https://carbonrobotics.com | **HQ:** Seattle, WA (manufacturing in Eastern Washington) | **Founded:** 2018 | **Stage/Funding:** Series D — $177M total raised ($70M Series D, Oct 2024; $20M Series D-2, Oct 2025); investors include BOND, NVentures (NVIDIA), Anthos Capital, Revolution

## Product
Carbon Robotics sells the LaserWeeder, a tractor-pulled implement that uses computer vision, deep-learning, and CO₂ lasers to identify and thermally destroy weeds at the meristem without herbicides. The original 20-foot model carries 30 lasers firing every 50ms. The February 2025 G2 line adds modular widths (20 ft / 40 ft / 60 ft) targeting row crops (soy, corn) alongside specialty crops; the G2 600 lists at ~$1.4M plus annual support fees. A companion Carbon Ops Center software platform provides fleet telemetry. In March 2025 the company unveiled the Carbon ATK (Autonomous Tractor Kit), an autonomous guidance retrofit for existing farm equipment.

## ICP
Large-acreage commercial growers in North America (primary), Western Europe, and Australia — specialty/high-value crops (leafy greens, onions, carrots, broccoli, herbs) where herbicide costs and residue restrictions are acute. The $1M+ price point self-selects for operations with 200+ acres under a given crop where payback runs 1–3 years. The G2 row-crop expansion broadens the ICP toward conventional grain and oilseed producers.

## Traction
100+ grower customers across 14 countries; 50,000+ operating hours logged in 2024; 10B+ weeds eliminated since 2022; revenue growing 3× YoY as of 2024 (absolute figure not disclosed). (Sources: The Robot Report, 2024; company press releases, 2025.)

## Tech moat
Proprietary deep-learning weed-vs-crop classification models trained on billions of in-field images since 2022 represent a meaningful data moat. Laser targeting hardware is custom-engineered. First-mover manufacturing scale in a capital-intensive category creates some cost-curve advantage. IP filings exist but are not extensively publicized; regulatory moat is thin (no pesticide registration analogue). Network effects are absent.

## Distribution model
Direct sales to growers; no channel or OEM partners disclosed. Capital equipment purchase model (outright ownership). The ATK may eventually enable a broader install-base play via retrofits, but current motion is direct enterprise sales with field support teams.

## Critical weakness
The $1M+ unit price walls off the vast majority of global farmland. Laser efficacy drops sharply on weeds taller than a few inches and does not kill perennial root systems, limiting use cases. Full dependency on tractor-tow means the product does not function on beds, greenhouses, or terraced/irregular terrain. Concentrated customer base (100+ growers, 14 countries) leaves revenue highly exposed to capital-expenditure freezes during commodity price downturns.

## Adjacent gap
Carbon Robotics is structurally locked out of smallholder and mid-size farms (under ~150 acres of specialty crops) by price, and out of all non-row, non-specialty terrain by form factor. A credible wedge: a **lower-capital-cost robotic weeding service (RaaS) targeting mid-size vegetable and soft-fruit growers in Europe** — where herbicide bans (EU Farm to Fork) are creating acute demand but $1M capex is unviable. The RaaS model converts the barrier from capex to opex, and EU regulatory tailwinds compress the sales cycle. No well-funded incumbent owns this motion.
